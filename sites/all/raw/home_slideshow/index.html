Students want to learn skills.

 

 

Universities want to help them...

 

 

...but don't always do a good job.

Mediocre courses. Students don't know what they need to.

 

Grads without good skills.

 

Employers can't fill well-paying jobs in programming, engineering, data analysis, accounting...

It doesn't have to be this way

 

We know how to help people learn skills. Existing research shows us how.

 

But universities don't use the research. Professors don't study it. They do research. Publish or perish.

Will universities rewire themselves to improve skills courses?

 

No. Not soon. Current practices are too entrenched.

 

But there are cracks in the system.

Cracks in the system

Some professors (maybe 10%) are willing to learn about learning. Let's help them make courses for other professors.

 

Most professors (maybe 80%) like interacting with students. Let's make their interaction effective.

 

Professors like using their skills (e.g., math professors like doing math). Let's help them do that.

 

More cracks in the system

Professors or committees choose textbooks. They can choose good ones, as well as bad.

 

It doesn't take a Ph.D. to grade intro programming exercises. There are people around the globe who can do it. Let's put them to work.

CyberCourse

CyberCourse (Cyco for short) is a philosophy, set of techniques, and open source software.

 

Cyco works through the cracks in the system. It can improve courses now, without waiting for universities to fix themselves.

Four roles

Authors: create courses

Professors: run courses

Students: learn

Graders: give feedback

 

It starts with authors...

Authors

Authors are experts in chemistry, analytics, writing, etc. They want to help students learn.

 

The Cyco community helps authors learn about learning. The basics, like outcome-based courses, deep learning, metacognition, and formative feedback.

Authors

Authors create CyberCourses. Cycos are like online textbooks, but written to higher quality standards.

 

"Quality" means students learning skills.

 

Less reading, more exercises, more feedback.

Authors

Open source Cyco software helps authors.

  • Course design tools link skills, knowledge, and activities (outcome-driven course design).
  • Grading tools with "clickable rubrics" support large-scale human grading (formative feeback).
  • Pseudostudents model good learning (metacognition).
  • And much more.

Self-sustaining

One Cyco can serve thousands of students.

 

Authors can sell access to their Cycos. They don't get 15% royalties. They get 100% of the revenue.

 

Authors can do good work, set low prices, and still pay the mortgage.

Courses matching students' needs

Authors can serve small markets.

 

For example, an algebra course for Spanish-speaking students in south Florida. Local language, local issues.

 

When the Miami Marlins play, game stats are in the course the next day.

Professors choose Cycos, and run courses based on them. Students learn skills.

Running CyberCourses

Flipped, blended, heavy on the assessment.

 

No lectures. With good content, there's no need.

 

Many exercises, perhaps three times as many as traditional courses.

Outside class time

Students read Cyco content independently.

 

They do exercises, and submit their work online.

 

They get feedback. Not just a grade, but a list of things they should improve.

What happens in class?

Professors work with students one-on-one, or in small groups.

 

Profs help students do tasks. Write programs, balance equations, analyze data...

 

Many profs prefer this to lecturing.

Timing

Students work through courses at their own pace, or at a pace set by the professor.

 

The software knows when students fall behind.

Four roles

Authors: create courses

Professors: run courses

Students: learn

Graders: give feedback

 

Graders give every student feedback on every exercise.

Graders

Humans. No automated grading.

 

Graders need enough expertise, but need not be Ph.Ds.

 

They can be local or remote.

 

Graders use clickable rubrics for efficiency.

No institutional change

Universities don't need to change the way they operate.

 

Semesters, classes, sections, credit hours, registration, schedules, grades... all can stay the same.

 

CyberCourse gives universities more flexibility, but they don't have to take advantage of it.
